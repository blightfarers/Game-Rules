Combat in Blight-Farers revolves around martial exchanges. A martial exchange occurs whenever one character takes aggressive action with real intent and another must respond or suffer the consequences. The structure of a martial exchange is not only mechanical - it is a frame for GM’s to build narration and combat transitions off of.

A character can typically only participate in a single martial exchange each round of combat. That’s their turn. A round lasts until each active combatant finishes taking their turn. Whether they participated offensively or defensively, that’s it for them for the round. The outcomes determine who leads the martial exchanges of the next round.

While you lead the initiative you are best set-up to put your opponent in a spot and to deal damage, or to intercede on someone's behalf. These general intentions are described as "Advancing Attitudes”. While you are on defense you are best set-up to protect yourself or another and re-gain control by intercepting your antagonists intentions. These are called “Responsive Attitudes”.

The combative decisions you make necessitate awareness of the environment, setting up valuable maneuvers, and disrupting your opponents ability to maintain their lead so that you can turn unfair and dangerous situations around - or use those principles to aid your allies. 

When a fight first starts, the first person to attack has seized the initiative. This means that their action is qualified and adjudicated as one of the Advancing Attitudes.

Instead of choosing an attitude, describe what your character does in the fiction. It will naturally fit into one of these frames for adjudication - the GM decides what attitude best describes your action.

When you are attacking a character who is mobile but is not or cannot give a focused response to your attack, the difficulty of the Check is 8 + Agility. If their armor has the Glancing trait, this also adds to their passive defense.

When you deal damage, roll the weapons damage or the damage for the type of attack, and add the appropriate Attribute.

When you take damage, before tallying it against your Wound Threshold, reduce it by your armor's Protection. As described in Core Concepts: Character Health, taking damage can cause you to lose Health Levels or to suffer additional status effects beyond what is explicitly described here.
Attacking: Advancing Attitudes
Simple Advance
You attempt something that is approachable for just about anyone. It is direct, simple, and effective. Attacking generally, initiating a grapple, shoving someone, or gaining a positional advantage (potentially: +2 Asset).

On Hit you accomplish a simple objective. 

On Triumph, your objective succeeds to its fullest extent. Simple objectives gain additional benefits such as extra damage, incidentally tripping or dazing them, and so forth.
Ambitious Advance
This action is difficult, complex, requires skilled coordination, or is otherwise challenging to perform (especially without previous set-up). This is commonly good for trips, joint locks, chokes, disarms, and so forth.

When you roll, suffer Disadvantage on the roll. The most common and simple counter to this penalty is first making a grab for your opponent - but taking a turn to start a grapple can also slow you down. It’s your choice.

On a Hit you accomplish the objective and maintain your initiative. On a Triumph you accomplish your objective cleanly and well.
Ready
You remain ready for violence but refuse to commit until necessary. Describe how you prepare yourself and wait for an opening. When you choose Ready, immediately relinquish the initiative.
Any character who would otherwise be responding to you may now claim the initiative and take an Advancing Attitude. They may also choose to Ready, or they might turn their attention to another exchange. If no one claims the initiative, the exchange ends. 
While Ready, the first time another character leads an exchange against you, gain a +2 Asset on your Responsive Attitude. Resolve your response normally as a Guard, Counter, or Heedless Response.
You cannot Ready while doing something else that requires your true focus (but you could deceive and pretend not to be Ready). You also cannot Ready against a persistent effect already affecting you (of course, you may Ready against an expected repeating effect, that’s different).
Advancing Attitude Modifiers
A Simple or Ambitious Advance may also be counted as a Reckless Advance. When it is, on a Hit your Advance benefits from its Triumph level effects instead, but you lose your initiative control in return. On a Triumph, keep your initiative control after all.

When you describe your offense, if it includes multiple simultaneous acts or several acts chained together in immediate succession, it may also be counted as a Split Advance. In this case for 2 actions roll each separately, with a 1 point Hindrance on each. For a Triple action, do the same but with a 3 point Hindrance instead. Either way, your opponent rolls once and compares that result against all rolls made as part of this Advance. You should roll your actions in order as one can affect the next.

An action can be both Reckless and Split, whether it is Simple or Ambitious, the effects still combine together seamlessly. Not all actions in a Split Advance need to be of the same type(s). If neither roll lets you keep your initiative, lose it.
Defending: Responsive Attitudes
When another character leads a martial exchange against you, there are several Responsive Attitudes you can take:
Guard Response
You focus entirely on survival or holding a position. You gain a +2 Asset on all defensive rolls during this round.

On a Miss, you suffer the effects of the attack as normal. On a Hit, you successfully defend yourself but gain no further benefit.

On a Triumph, choose one:
Seize the initiative for the next exchange.
You may react to additional opponents this round. Each time you do reduce the Asset bonus from Ready by 1. If you cannot, you can take no further reactions this round.

Example:
On your initial Guard roll you have a +2 Asset for focusing fully on defense. You rolled a Triumph. You chose the 2nd option. Another enemy also tried to attack you. Your +2 Asset still applies, but after this roll, reduce it by 1. After the 3rd enemy attacks you, reduce it by 1 again to 0. You cannot react to a 4th enemy.
Counter Response
You defend yourself while simultaneously pursuing another secondary goal. You might evade into a better angle of attack, parry & riposte, throw sand into an opponent's eyes, or perform any number of tactical maneuvers while protecting yourself.

Counter combines defending yourself with a secondary objective. Decide whether Simple or Ambitious Advance best describes your secondary goal. It may not be Wild Advance (see Gamble), Split Advance (this is already a Split action), or Ready (see Guard to go on full defense).

Both combatants roll. On a Hit, you successfully defend yourself, but that’s it.

On a Triumph, you successfully defend yourself and gain the Hit benefit of your Advance immediately.
Heedless Response
You abandon sound defense in favor of immediate action. Perhaps you panic and flee, launch yourself into a desperate attack, charge through danger, or attempt something knowingly risky in hopes of turning the situation around. You are no longer tactically defending yourself. 

If you roll a Miss, their action lands and yours fails. If you roll a Hit, their action lands when it normally would not, but so does yours. Both actions are resolved as if they were a Hit, but you seize the initiative.

If you roll a Triumph, your action interrupts theirs. Your act succeeds as if it were a Hit, but without suffering the consequences of their attack and you seize the initiative, leading your next exchange with them.
Combat Statuses
Vulnerable - Vulnerable characters lose initiative if they have it, and take Disadvantage on all actions.

Grappling - you have Advantage against a target you are grappling.
