# Setting Introduction

Again and again civilization has fallen to the temptations of darkness and brought upon themselves their own curse. From the shattered lands of Drenor where oblivion crawls forth from dark portals to Dral’Kannum where greed and desperation brought about the treaty that plunders the world's most primal lands, Hylandia where those few nations that remain were brought to their knees through wartorn infighting, or Estaron - the first land, a paradise named after the world itself - now blighted from sea to sea with undeath by Mazut before ascending as the lich-god, leaving only pockets of society remaining.

You are a sojourner in those blighted lands, surrounded by danger on every turn, the hunger of both the living and the damned, forced to spend each day toiling for the scraps of survival. Yet, with perhaps a justifiable defense for despair, you continue anyways. Years before all of mortality was separated from divinity, and Estaron walled off from the rest of the world. Yet you remain.

Who are you? Why do you yet endure? Who will you become?

This is the first installment of a line of roleplaying games where players survive terrible conditions and play through what changes they can wreak in the world. The lore of this world is deep and explorable. It starts beyond the stars, is built around them, and eventually beyond those limitless bounds it will return. The danger of this world is real - but despite those conditions, so is its hope. Discover how and why for yourself as you play Blight Farers: Liches Killed the Gods™.

At the start of the game you will hop into a tutorial session that teaches you the basics of play. During this tutorial, or at least before the next session, you fill out your character sheet and finalize details about who they are and where they are most competent. As you make your character, you need to understand what you will be facing.

Blight Farers is a TTRPG that focuses on creative thinking, working against constraints, and navigating within dilemmas. Everything has its impact. Your character can potentially be anyone and become anything. That is, if they can make it through the day.

Because of the dynamics which you will both suffer and enjoy in this game, think of it like a game of heroic survival. Everything is against you. You’ll have to manage yourself well. And you’ll need to take care of your team. Inconsiderate care and betrayal often lead to significant loss. But, simultaneously to all that, you're not a victim - you're an agent in a dark world that’s trying to take advantage of you. 

There will always be something you can do, a shred of hope to draw on against all odds, and even something to enjoy in the darkest of times. In fact, the gloom you face on your adventures is secretly a miracle, and one day that deeper light will rise from obscurity. Your darkest games will be a fond memory. And maybe even some personal practice.

If that is what your gaming table experiences, you're playing Blight Farers well.

As you make decisions about your character, or as they make choices under pressure with competing goals, who they are in the world and in the story becomes more real. You will not spend hours up front dedicated solely to a starting character - but as they continue, they will also grow.

In order to support the constraints of a survival TTRPG alongside the flexibility needed for stories of despair and transformation the game engine implements principle based structure - aimed to provide constraints and drive behavior while simultaneously leaving the door open for ingenuity, creativity, and GM fiat.

With all of that in mind, this game will ask both players and GMs to think a bit differently from traditional TTRPGs. This framework starts with Character Competencies

Core Rules
Character Competencies




Backgrounds
Everyone has an origin - something that is part of their personal history before the start of the game. This is called your character Background. 

In short, everyone had a homeland, community, & skillset.

Your Background includes at least two details, one that describes who you were or what you were doing, and another that describes the circumstances surrounding you during that era. It would typically be written as "[Profession or Archetype] of [Location, Community, Event]” or otherwise includes the same details.

Examples include:

Refugee of the Hylandian Wars
Pirate of the Azure Seas
Horizon Chasers Guild, Wilderness Guide
Arkel-Leuom Street Sweeper
Demon Hunter from The Shattered Isles
Blackwater League Merchant

You can actually have multiple Backgrounds. Each will have a Rank associated with it. Its Rank gets added to any rolls where the skills, knowledge, attention to detail, or sociality seem applicable. If more than one Background applies, each adds their Rank to the roll. 

Your Backgrounds can even have shared experience. If you have 2 Ranks in being a Wilderness Guide for the traveling merchants in Hylandia, that doesn’t preclude you from being a Wilderness Guide for someone or somewhere else, or from also having been a caravan guard. A guide might not have done much fighting, but a guard would have. You could have been both.

Without a feature counting your character as Trained they are, by default, Untrained. This means you’ll suffer a 2 point Hindrance on the roll for common things like swimming, fighting, or negotiating a sale. For especially complex, intricate, or unique skills (like picking locks, an advanced martial maneuver, or casting a spell without training) you’ll instead take Disadvantage. 

Whenever a Background applies, you're automatically Trained, even if it’s only Rank 1. An individual Background may not have more than 3 Ranks. You must have at least one Background with at least 1 Rank in it.

Talents
You can also learn and specialize in specific skills rather than simply develop broad proficiency. As you develop individual skills specifically you can gain Ranks in a Talent. If a Talent applies you are Trained and it adds its Rank to your Check.

A Talent may not have more than 3 Ranks.

That's the basics of character competencies. But, how competent you are hardly matters if you can’t survive.

Character Health
Your health can severely impact how effective you are - no matter how skilled you are. In Blight Farers, the struggle against death is a game of cat and mouse. How well you pace yourself and coordinate for the well-being of your whole team matters exceedingly.

Health Track
You have a Health Track which has 5 Health Levels. When something is impactful enough or has built up enough it leads to you losing one of the Levels in your Health Track. If you’ve only got two Health Levels or less remaining you are Battered.

Battered: suffer Disadvantage on all actions that are not about defending, disengaging, suing for peace, or otherwise focused defensively on keeping you alive. If you would become Battered while already so, become Vulnerable instead until one of the effects causing you to be Battered ends.

You will typically lose a Health Level from the cascading effects of Ailing Conditions or from taking damage.

Taking Damage - Wound Threshold
When you successfully hit with an attack, you may deal damage by rolling your weapon damage and adding the appropriate Attribute - usually Power or Agility, or Potency for spells.

When you take damage, track it on your character sheet beside your Wound Threshold (WT). Your WT equals 2 + Vitality + (½ Spirit).

Whenever accumulated damage equals or exceeds your WT, lose one Health Level then subtract WT from the damage total that your tracking. If sufficient damage remains you may have to repeat this process multiple times.

For example, if you have a WT of 3 and take 5 damage, you’ll lose a health level and tally the other 2 points of damage. If you previously had taken 1 damage, instead you’ll end up losing 2 Health Levels with no tally’s for damage remaining.

Ailing Conditions - Struggling for The Minimums
When it comes to your Ailing Conditions, you’ve got 3 to stay aware of - Tired, Hungry, & Distressed. These are binary states; either you are Hungry, or you're not. While you have an Ailing Condition, put a mark in its checkbox. When you get to remove that condition, erase that mark.

Additionally, if you would have to mark an Ailing Condition while you already have it marked, the effect Cascades, requiring you to mark one of the other two Ailing Conditions. You choose which. If you cannot because all of them are marked, you lose a Health Level instead.

None of the Ailing Conditions apply any penalties while you have them. They are a warning and a buffer - a signal that you are functionally nearing degraded health.

You mark Tired if you didn’t get at least poor sleep (~ more than 4 hours) or you Fumble a check for Endurance. 
You mark Hungry if you either didn’t hydrate or get at least 2 poor meals (rations) during the day.
You mark Distressed when you fail a Check for morale such as due to violence, horror, despair, or deep disgust.

Sometimes your GM will make note of special circumstances that help you flourish and ask you to put a star beside one of the Ailing Conditions. While you have an Ailing Condition starred, the next time you would mark it, remove the star instead.

Each starred condition grants a +1 Asset to your Daily Recovery Check and to healing checks made by your caretakers.

If you had plenty of sleep (~9+ hours) in comfortable conditions, or took a day off from adventure in a place of safety, you should star Tired.
If you had a filling and hearty meal or ate well during feasts & festivities, you should star Hungry.
If you are exceedingly prepared for the path ahead of you, are especially faithfully centered & determined, or uplifted by the true companionship of your fellows, you should star Distressed.

If you have an ailing condition marked but would get to star it, instead remove the mark. This is the primary way to clear marked ailing conditions.

Natural Recovery & Medical Aid
Each dawn you make a Vitality Check for daily recovery with a difficulty of 13. On a Hit, restore one lost Health Level. On a Triumph restore 2. With a Fumble, you have contracted a minor illness and must Lock Up a Health Level. If dawn occurs during an encounter, the GM will have you make this check either right before or right after.

This timeline is not random, & not merely part of a daily cycle. The world is striving to heal itself - and that includes you. In certain areas of the world the difficulty of this check may be greater or lesser than normal.

Additionally, a caretaker or physician may make checks, with time and necessary resources, to tend to the needs of the suffering. 

To clear tracked damage is a quick process, and simple bandages, wraps, and the like can easily be improvised. No check is required so long as the caretaker has the skill and ability to focus & work for a short time, for each patient, and they have basic resources.

A caretaker or physician may not restore lost Health Levels, but over the course of repeated care and monitoring may help ease your recovery. 

As a character receives care specific to their needs and that care is maintained across at least several hours their caretaker may make a difficulty 13 check for healing. On a Hit, the patient gains a +2 Asset on their next Daily Recovery Check and Sickness Checks. On a Triumph they gain Advantage in addition to the +2.

Special effects & circumstances may modify the difficulty of checks for healing like normal. Tools and time are almost always required, although some resources may be improvised. Plus, filth and infection love to get in the way when they can.

Sickness
Sickness and toxins are not a survival condition - they often have their own special effects, apply a Hindrance, mark Ailing Conditions, or directly cause the loss of a Health Level. Every illness can be different, and even cause dilemmas or provoke adventures. They may have certain conditions before they can be removed and may worsen from day to day or prevent recovery.

Many illnesses are minor and have a common, generic effect. You might take a universal 1 point Hindrance or have to Lock Up a Health Level.

The Hindrance applies to all checks focused on social, perceptive, mental, or physical activity, as well as to your own Daily Recovery, and the difficulty for physicians to heal you. When you would recover a Health Level, instead cure this illness.

If you must Lock Up a Health Level, draw a box around one of your Health Levels - while it is locked up it cannot be lost nor healed, play the game as if you had one less Health Level in your Health Track than normal. Next time you would recover a Health Level, instead Unlock the Health Level by erasing the box. The Unlocked Health Level stays in the same state it was when it got Locked up - if you had the Health Level you have it now and if it was lost when it was locked up it must still be recovered separately.

Dying & Death Checks
If you lose all 5 Health Levels you are incapacitated and dying. At the end of the encounter you will make a Death Check - the base difficulty is 13, and you’ll roll the greater between Spirit or Vitality.

On a Fumble, lose your 6th health level. On a Miss, you’re still unstable. The next time you would roll for Daily Recovery instead make a new Death Check. On a Hit, you stabilize. You're still incapacitated & don’t regain any lost Health Levels yet, but this trial won’t be the one to lay you low. On a Triumph, benefit from the effects of a Hit plus you gain Advantage on your next Daily Recovery Check.

If you lose a 6th health level, you die. Your soul will visit the Hall of Journeys, you will have clarity about your life and potential, and there you may return home as you pass through the doors of eternity. 

The harsh conditions that have affected the world and the struggles of survival have also altered magic itself. Since the displacement of the gods, revival magic has fled from the world. Only a few methods of revival remain, and are not commonly accessible.
Magic of Estaron
Despite the implications of the fragmentation of divine magic, magic at large is still an important part of the world of Estaron. People eventually find ways to adapt, survive, and even thrive. At least until something more comes around to put them down again.

Throughout history there have been many magical theories and traditions but at the root of it all a mage must be able to attune to the forces of magic, channel it by their mind & will, and withstand the consequences that come to mortals from wielding such powers themselves.

At the heart of all magic lies Mana. Mana is the ethereal energy that flows through and around all things. Throughout history countless philosophies have attempted to explain its nature and how it may best be wielded. Some manipulate it through instinct, others through careful study, experimentation, and practice, but every mage ultimately draws upon the same supernatural force.

The way Mana is accessed varies between traditions. Some mages carry it within themselves, while others prepare vessels, techniques, or rituals that allow them to safely channel the same force. 

A mage's Mana Pool represents the amount of Mana they can safely draw upon, contain, and direct before their body and spirit begin suffering the consequences. When your Mana Pool is at 0, you may continue casting spells, but doing so forces you to bear the cost yourself.

Your magical tradition outlines how your Mana Pool works.
Casting Spells
Each spell is a specific magical effect with its own Mana cost, associated magical domains, and unique effects. To cast a spell, spend Mana equal to its Mana cost, then make a Spellcasting Check as you feed the shape the creative forces of the world into being.

On a Hit, the spell succeeds and resolves its effects as described. On a Miss the Mana is spent but the spell does not take effect.

If you cannot pay a spell's full Mana Cost, spend as much Mana as you have remaining. Then suffer Mana Feedback once for each unpaid Mana.

Your magical tradition prescribes which Attribute you use to cast your spells.
Empowering Spells
Most spells have optional upgrades listed with it called Empowerments - effectively making each spell its own modifiable toolkit. 

As you cast a spell you can choose to upgrade it by paying the additional mana cost of one or more Empowerments and adding their Hindrance into your Spellcasting Check.

If your spell succeeds, the spell resolves normally but with additional or modified effects as described in the Empowerment. If an Empowered spell fails, suffer Mana Feedback once.
Mana Limit
Power is not only limited by how much Mana you possess, but by how much you can safely channel at once.

Your Mana Limit is equal to [Spellcasting Attribute + Spirit].

At the end of each of your turns, total all Mana you spent during that turn. If the amount spent exceeds your Mana Limit, suffer Mana Feedback once for each point above your Mana Limit.

If you cannot pay a spell's full Mana Cost and you exceed your Mana Limit the effects stack - meaning you can end up doubling up on Mana Feedback.

Magical Traditions
Spell Blood
Your magic is not based on training, practice, or study. Magic is part of your blood. It's instinct and intention driven by your will -and the world itself responds. The Attribute you use for Spellcasting Checks is Spirit.

You have a Mana Pool equal to [Vitality + Potency + Spirit * 2]. You regain 1d6 Mana +1 for each Starred Ailing Condition every time you roll Daily Recovery.

You know a number of spells at character creation equal to your Intuition.
Awakened
Your magic is born from deep attunement to the infinite possibilities found within your mind, soul, and the world around you. Whether through qi, psionics, or supernatural harmony with the primordial forces of nature, you shape reality by entering states of profound resonance rather than through study or instinct alone.

Your Mana Pool represents your Resonance—the harmony you have cultivated with these greater forces. You recover Mana through methods unique to your tradition.

Awakened mages possess Mantles: distinct states of body, mind, and spirit that bring them into accord with particular supernatural principles. While a Mantle is active, you gain its passive benefit and may cast its associated spells by spending Mana. You may have only one Mantle active at a time.

Your Spellcasting Attribute is Intuition.

Spell Wright
Your magic is diligently earned through consistent practice, study, experimentation, and refinement. You draw on the ambient energies of the world around you to summon up Mana for your spells and utilize contraptions and enchantments to directly support your spell casting. The Attribute you use for Spellcasting Checks is Reason.

Your Mana Pool is 0, and never gains Mana on its own. Instead you Gather Mana as an action.

Gather Mana: roll 2d10 + Reason. Divide the total by 4, ignoring any remainder. Add the result to your Mana Pool. Gathering Mana requires a full turn or is done freely as part of activating a Mana Anchor.
Mana Overflow
You can safely hold only so much Mana. If your Mana Pool ever exceeds twice your Mana Limit, immediately interrupt the current action. Your Mana Anchor collapses, all Mana in your Mana Pool is lost, and any spell you were casting fails before taking effect. This is called Mana Overflow.
Spell Harness
Additionally, Spell Wrights have developed a unique ability to construct a Spell Harness to help protect themselves and enhance the spells they specialize in most.

A Spell Harness is a magical framework established to make casting powerful spells more stable. There are many different Spell Harnesses, and each is learned separately from actual spells. 

Each Spell Harness is also tied to one or more magical domains - keywords that signify what supernatural forces it is built for such as Shadow, Earth, or Gravity. The benefits of your Spell Harness only applies to spells you cast that share at least one domain with the Harness. For example, a Water domain Harness does not give its benefits to Fire domain spells.

Generating a Spell Harness has a Mana Cost and is a single action, but its benefits don’t apply until the end of the turn where you initiated it. If you cannot pay its full Mana cost, suffer Mana Feedback for each unpaid Mana to activate it,otherwise the Harness collapses and the action is wasted.

When you create a Spell Harness, make a Spellcasting Check with a difficulty of 13. On a Miss the Harness creation fails. On a Hit it forms and provides a +1 Asset. On a Triumph it instead provides a +2 Asset, and you regain Mana equal to its Mana Cost.

While your Mana Harness remains active:

You gain the Assets granted when you created the Harness on Spellcasting Checks for spells that share its domain.
So long as your Empowerment Hindrances are less than or equal to your Harness Assets, do not suffer Mana Feedback from an Empowered Spell failing.
Spells with a matching domain cost 1 less Mana to cast.

You may have only one Harness active at a time. You may dismiss it freely. Creating a new Spell Harness replaces your current one.

A Spell Harness may remain active through ordinary activity. Whenever a significant passage of time occurs, such as travel, exploration, or rest, maintaining it requires paying its Mana cost again. If you cannot pay this cost, you may continue maintaining the Harness by suffering Mana Feedback once for each Mana you could not pay.

Your Mana Harness draws the essence of its domain into the world around you, causing the magic you wield to become visible and tangible. Embers may drift through the air, the earth may seem unnaturally steady beneath your feet, or nearby shadows may deepen and stir.

Where violence, suspicion, or fear are concerns, maintaining a Spell Harness is much like openly carrying a loaded gun. Others can plainly see that you are prepared to wield powerful magic, and that should make them wary.

From competencies to health & magic, these core concepts and patterns set the frame for the game. You’ll still have equipment, lineage traits, and more than fills out your character and handles the nuances of the game - but from here you’ll understand how the dynamics of the system work together to invite play in a difficult world.

Rhythm of The Session
Your game leader will present opportunities to engage with the world. As you do, quests will reveal themselves that will include opportunities to connect, explore, fight, and solve. 

Scenarios
Each game session is episodic and presents a scenario, multiple smaller scenarios (think: Tales of Ba Sing Se), or a single act of a greater scenario. That scenario has a dramatic question that identifies the defining condition as to when the scenario reaches its conclusion, regardless of how. 

Once the answer to a dramatic question is clear and unavoidable, the scenario is brought to a close. The GM will narrate through the final moments and transition to the next stage. Answering the dramatic question favorably is the primary objective of the player characters.

Session Loop
Most of the game follows a simple pattern:

The GM sets the stage & presents a scenario, then clearly invites players to act.
The players ask questions, investigate, explore, and declare actions. 
The GM adjudicates actions. When appropriate the dice are rolled. The roll total helps determine the results and the game leader translates those into the narrative.
The GM describes how things change and transitions the focus to the next moment of opportunity.

Ending The Session
As the session is brought to a close, the table answers these questions together:

What happened?
What do we want to remember? What did we learn? Make note of these things.
What are we doing next in the game?

Assigning XP
Next, the GM assigns equal XP to each party member. They gain 1 XP when the scenario is brought to a close. This is the completion of the quest, for better or for worse. Answering it victoriously grants 1 additional XP. This is completing it well. Further XP can be gained under the following conditions based on your GM’s feeling:

If you suffered no major losses, gain 1 XP
If you found optional content, gain 1 XP
If you completed optional scenario objectives, gain 1 XP
If you exemplified teamwork, gain 1 XP

Prepare For Next Session
Before the players each go their separate ways, the table spends no more than 5 minutes loosely scheduling in the most likely dates for the next two gaming sessions and grants the GM permission to follow-up with each member of the table in advance of those dates.

The table may also briefly discuss other plans or back-up plans as necessary, including sub-plots, other games for game night, budget-friendly local real-life activities, serving, uplifting, or celebrating someone at the table, or rescheduling an upcoming session.

Finally, the GM takes simple notes on the scenario:

Bullet point events from the session. What happened and when? Or at least, what order?
Bullet point NPCs from the session. Who did the players meet (what are they known as, and what “character background” phrase best describes them) and where? 
What can you bring full circle later (sooner or later) by bringing rewards and consequences back around to the players directly due to this session?

These notes, as well as clearly knowing what the player characters will be doing next are used by the GM later for planning the next session.

Creator’s Postword
Behind the scenes this game aims to explore several questions and principles - things I am personally studying out & learning to put my feelings into words. These are:

No matter what, there is always something you can say, do, learn, or practice. 
If you don’t consciously & actively choose what you focus on, something else will.
How can something horrible, terrifying, or even catastrophic be part of something wonderful?
How can I tell the difference between something that seems good but is actually a distraction or a sinkhole, and something that is truly a worthy influence in the decisions & attitudes of my life?
What is it that makes hope and unfeigned goodness intelligent & strategic - not merely virtuous?

During my childhood at times we would be affected by the winds of hurricanes and other severe storms even though we were rarely in the most directly impacted areas. Our family would go out for walks during these and other lighter storms. These family walks are very positive memories of my earlier years - from watching trees fall and feeling the impact of the crash through the ground to swimming through minor floods and the clean up & community help that came afterwards - I learned to enjoy, explore, play, and uplift in a place where most others would, quite reasonably, rather not step outside.

Later on this became an analogy for me - where many see doom and dread I was taught to experience hope and enthusiasm. I wanted to share that through one of my favorite hobbies - tabletop roleplaying.

While this is first and foremost a game, I love when people take things from games that are useful to real life. I have often felt that wholesome play is one of the best strengtheners for mind, body, spirit, and community. So, I hope that as you play through your characters' challenges - as unfair as the game is designed to make them feel - that you get a little something to help you in your life as well, even if it’s just comfort, rest, and friendship. Or practice.

Your storms may be terrible, but they are a reason to get hopeful and enthusiastic, a sign of something greater to come. And a miracle all on their own. Don’t be so distracted by your storms that you forget to set your sights on what really matters. You are the person who has the brutal opportunity to make the best of your “beneficial” adversaries and to find rest in your storms. That may be an unfair challenge - but there's value and purpose to the principle.

I look forward and upward, to you, Storm Walker.
